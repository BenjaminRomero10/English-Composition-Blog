Ethics in cybersecurity

How to make cybersecurity ethics classes more entertaining?

November 18, 2022

Although values are taught to us at first in our homes, ethics are learned in study, in experiences, at school, institute or university. These ethical values are what will define us as people when working or acting together with another person or company. Learning ethics has a value beyond "being a good person", it is about knowing how to act in different cases, and it is about doing the morally correct thing, where all parties benefit, without affecting each other. Nowadays, students do not pay attention to moral ethics classes or they find it boring, that is why this blog will present tips to make classes more entertaining, so that our students understand the importance of ethics in cybersecurity

In this blog we will focus on the principles to engage students in cybersecurity ethics courses, guided mainly by the article by Bustard (2018) in the United Kingdom, analyzing them and giving examples of how to apply them in daily classes.

Principles for promoting engagement in cybersecurity ethics classes

As mentioned above, there are certain principles for engaging students in these ethics courses, specifically ethics in cybersecurity, which consist of (1) matching the learning content to the tastes of the students, (2) taking a pragmatic rather than a philosophical approach to issue resolution, (3) explore all the complexity in real-world case studies, and (4) present the content in a way that the student finds entertaining (Bustard, 2018).

Personally, I believe that these requirements are necessary today to form classes with greater harmony when it comes to learning, since they prioritize the fact that students feel that the content is closely aligned with the subject field.

A cybersecurity expert should focus on minimizing risks and maximizing benefits, looking for vulnerabilities and not taking advantage of them, but reporting and helping to improve them. The point of this type of ethical course is for future professionals to adopt this way of working, therefore every good professional should give the same importance as any other learning.

Next, we will go into depth with each of the principles proposed above.

1. Matching the learning content to the tastes of the students

It refers to aligning or selecting content relevant to the interests of the student. One of the ways to increase student engagement is to find interesting content for them. It is normal for a student not to see cybersecurity ethics classes as entertaining when they do not fully know what topics it covers, therefore they ignore it by saying "Why should I study this if I am an ethical person?

It is understandable that not all students share the same tastes among themselves, however there is always a topic in trend, which can be used to align it with a topic of study for the course.

A clear example of this would be to take advantage when a theme of magicians becomes trending and they could be aligned with the colored hats that represent the types of cybersecurity specialists that exist, as presented in Formosa, Wilson & Richards (2021):

...can be undertaken internally within an organisation or externally by authorised cybersecurity firms (white hat), by unauthorised hackers seeking bug bounties and other rewards without intending to harm organisations (grey hat), and by hackers seeking to damage organisations and exploit vulnerabilities (black hat) (p. 7).

2. Taking a pragmatic rather than a philosophical approach to issue resolution

Student learning should be derived more from examples than from theory. Cybersecurity ethics courses are created for the purpose of the student understanding and applying them in their working life, but it can become complicated by not knowing how to do it specifically. This is where examples come into play. To make it more interactive, you can even do a role dynamic, so that the students put themselves in the place of the character.

As much as today's youth are attached to technology, they are not aware of what a bad practice of ethics can cause or how to avoid being victims of it. One proof is the study carried out by Kalu, Chidi-Kalu, Okidi & Usiedo (2020) in Nigeria, where they questioned 150 young people to answer questions regarding cybersecurity and lack of ethics, where they conclude that that the ignorance in young people about cybersecurity is enormous, despite becoming victims of it and being knowledgeable about new technologies, with which I agree with it. This is where ethics comes in and could help us avoid these frauds since with ethics there would be no people trying to steal information or access someone else's personal things. In addition, we also highlight the importance of knowledge in cybersecurity, although we do not want to give up in this field of work, it is always good to take into account some basic concepts that can help us avoid misfortunes in everyday life.

This is what a lack of practice causes, less effect in real life and more stress trying to memorize the theory

3. Explore all the complexity in real-world case studies

Although we spoke before concentrating more on practice than on theory, it should be noted that practice, due to the very fact that it plays an important role when it comes to learning ethics in cybersecurity, must be based on real cases. We cannot continue giving cases where surreal things happen that will only happen 1 in 1000 times.

In addition to this, the case study must be comprehensive. This means that the case should not only be analyzed from an engineering point of view, but also from a political, social and even economic point of view in some cases. This is because when ethics is studied, regardless of whether it is in cybersecurity or in any other subject, we seek to make the student not only a better engineer, but also a better person. We can even see that ethics in cybersecurity does not have the same meaning in all fields, such as technology and social science, as Manjikian explains in a book review by Christen, Gordijn, & Loi (2020):

‘cybersecurity ethics’ itself has not actually meant the same thing to the two disparate audiences – technical practitioners and scholars in the social sciences... For a social scientist, cybersecurity is most often understood as a subset of national security concerns and therefore ethical, moral and legal questions are most often read within an existing set of understandings about national security and national security policymaking (p. 403).

4. Present the content in a way that the student finds entertaining

As we have discussed in previous points, there is no better way to teach than with interactive dynamics, in which students can laugh, be entertained and learn at the same time. This is one of the many ways in which the student can better grasp the content to be taught by the teacher.

Of the different advantages of teaching in a way that students find fun, it can be highlighted that in this type of activity, in addition to learning better, links and contacts are formed between the teacher and the student, and where there are links , there is appreciation and where there is appreciation there is connection.


I hope these tips have been useful for future classes in cybersecurity ethics, since today's youth have to understand the importance that this topic has in their professional and personal development. They are the future of our planet and we have to make sure that their teaching is of quality and with memorable memories, thank you very much!
